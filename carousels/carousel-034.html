Step 01 · Warm Up First
Engage before
you ever send a message.
Cold outreach fails because it skips the relationship stage.

Before you DM anyone, spend 1–2 weeks engaging with their content — genuinely. Leave thoughtful comments. Agree, push back, add insight. Like their posts. React to their updates.

When your name keeps appearing in their notifications, you're no longer a stranger. You're a familiar presence. That shift — from cold to warm — is the only thing that makes a DM feel welcome instead of intrusive.

Rule: Never send a DM to someone whose content you haven't engaged with at least 3 times in the past 14 days.

Step 02 · The Opening Line
Never lead
with an offer.
The first message sets the entire tone of the relationship.

The worst DMs start with: "Hi, I noticed your profile and I'd love to tell you about my service…" This is transactional. It signals you want something from them before you've earned any goodwill.

Start with a genuine, specific reference to something they said or shared. "Your post on [topic] last week actually shifted how I think about [X]…" This shows you paid attention. It's rare. It gets read.

Template: "Hey [Name] — your take on [specific post/idea] was genuinely interesting. Especially the part about [specific detail]."

Step 03 · Curiosity Trigger
Make them curious
enough to reply.
A great second message creates a knowledge gap they want closed.

Once they've replied to your opener, your next message should plant a curiosity seed — not deliver a pitch. Reference something you've observed about their situation or a question that implies you have relevant insight.

"I've been thinking about what you said about [X]. I've seen this play out a certain way with a few founders I work with — curious if you've run into the same thing?"

This invites dialogue. They engage to learn what you know. You're not selling — you're drawing them in.

Step 04 · Moving to Value
Give before
you ask for anything.
Value exchange is what separates conversations from transactions.

By message 3–4, share something genuinely useful — a resource, a framework, an insight specific to their situation. Not a lead magnet. Not a free trial. A real, no-strings insight that helps them right now.

"Based on what you shared about [problem], here's what I've seen work consistently: [framework/insight]. Thought this might be useful for where you are right now."

This positions you as the expert who gives. Not the salesperson who takes.

Rule: Give 2–3 rounds of genuine value before any soft ask. Trust is built in layers.

Step 05 · The Soft Ask
Ask for a call,
not a commitment.
The goal of a DM is a conversation — not a closed deal.

When the conversation has warmed, make a low-friction ask. Not "let me show you my proposal." Not "I'd love to work with you." Instead: "Would it be useful to jump on a 20-minute call? I can walk you through exactly how I'd approach [their problem]."

This is framed as value for them, not a sales call for you. It removes pressure. A soft ask at the right moment — after genuine engagement and value — converts at dramatically higher rates than any cold pitch.

Key: The ask should feel like a natural next step in the conversation, not a sudden gear change.
