Part 01 · The Hook
Line 1 is everything.
If they don't click "see more", nothing else matters.

LinkedIn shows 2–3 lines before the cut-off. Your first sentence must create enough curiosity, tension, or surprise that clicking "see more" feels involuntary.
The best hooks: make a bold claim, ask a question that stings, or open a story mid-action.

Examples: "I lost my biggest client. Here's what it taught me." / "Nobody talks about this LinkedIn mistake."

Part 02 · The Context
Set the scene fast.
Who, what, when — in 2 lines max.

After the hook, orient the reader quickly. Where does this story or insight take place? Who is involved? What was the situation?
Context is the bridge between the hook and the payoff. Keep it short — 1–3 sentences. Drag it out and you lose them.

Goal: Make the reader feel "this is relevant to me" within 30 words.

Part 03 · The Conflict
Introduce the problem
or turning point.
This is what makes them lean in.

Every compelling post has tension. A mistake made. A belief challenged. A result that surprised. The conflict is the emotional engine of the post.
Without conflict, your content is just information. With it, your content is a story — and stories get shared.

Tip: The conflict should feel uncomfortable. If it doesn't challenge something, it won't stick.

Part 04 · The Insight
Deliver the payoff.
The lesson. The revelation. The framework.

This is why they read the whole post. The insight is your expertise made visible. It answers: "What do I take away from this?"
Be specific. Be direct. Don't hedge. A vague takeaway wastes everything that came before it. Say exactly what you learned or what you'd do differently.

Rule: One post, one insight. Don't dilute it with five lessons — pick the best one.

Part 05 · The CTA
Tell them the next step.
Every. Single. Time.
No CTA = no clients.

Your CTA doesn't have to be a sales pitch. Ask a question. Invite a comment. Point to a resource. Offer a free call.
What it cannot be: nothing. Ending a post without direction is leaving money on the table. The algorithm rewards engagement. A CTA drives it.

Best CTA formats: "What would you add?" / "DM me X" / "Save this for later."

Post Anatomy

The complete structure — at a glance

Hook
First 2–3 Lines
Bold claim, open loop, or story-in-motion. Force the "see more" click.
Context
Scene Setting (1–3 sentences)
Who, what, where. Make it feel relevant immediately.
Conflict
The Tension or Turning Point
The mistake, challenge, or surprising discovery. This is the emotional engine.
Insight
The Payoff — One Clear Lesson
Your expertise distilled. Specific, direct, memorable. Never vague.
CTA
One Clear Next Step
Question, offer, resource, or invitation. Always give a reason to act.
This structure works for stories, tips, opinions, and lessons. Master it once. Use it forever.
