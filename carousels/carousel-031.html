Element 01
A hook that creates
an unfinished loop.
The brain hates open loops. Use that.

The most-shared posts open a question or tension in line 1 that the reader desperately wants to resolve. "I was about to quit LinkedIn. Then this happened." You cannot scroll past that. The brain demands closure — and clicking "see more" is how it gets it.
Your hook should promise a payoff. Then deliver.

Test: Cover your post after line 1. Does the reader need to know what comes next? If not, rewrite the first line.

Element 02
Emotional truth
over polished advice.
People share what makes them feel something.

The highest-shared posts aren't the most technically correct. They're the most emotionally accurate. When someone reads your post and thinks "this is exactly how I feel" — that's the share trigger.
Vulnerability, frustration, surprise, pride, and humour are the emotions that spread. Tips don't spread. Feelings do.

Ask yourself: What would someone feel reading this? If the answer is "nothing in particular" — rewrite with emotional honesty.

Element 03
One sharp, specific
insight — not five.
Depth beats breadth every time.

The instinct when writing is to share everything you know. Resist it. A post that makes one point with total conviction and clarity outperforms a list of ten shallow tips.
Your reader should finish your post with one idea lodged in their brain. If they finish with ten semi-ideas, they remember zero. Go deep, not wide. One knife, not a handful of spoons.

Element 04
Write to be shared
by a specific person.
Who would tag someone in this? Write for them.

Before writing, ask: "Who would share this and who would they tag?" The best viral posts are written for the person who reads it AND the person they'll send it to.
"Tag a founder who needs to read this." That's not an accident — it's architecture. Write so specifically that someone feels the urge to forward it to someone who needs it right now.

Element 05
Post when your
audience is active.
Timing amplifies content. Don't waste it.

A great post at 11pm on Sunday is dead content. The LinkedIn algorithm measures early engagement velocity. If your first hour generates 10 comments, the algorithm shows it to 5× more people. If it gets 0, it stops.
Best windows: Tuesday–Thursday, 7–9am or 12–2pm (your audience's timezone). Post, then spend 30 minutes replying to every comment immediately.

The first 60 minutes matter most. Be available. Reply fast. Fuel the algorithm with your own engagement.
