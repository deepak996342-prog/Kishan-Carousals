Signal 01 · Specialisation
The specialist always
commands more than
the generalist.
Narrowing your niche increases perceived value, not decreases it.

Generalists say: "I help businesses grow." Specialists say: "I help Series A SaaS companies cut churn by 30% in 90 days." Which one can charge 5× more?

Premium positioning starts with radical specificity. The more precisely you define who you serve and what result you produce, the more expensive and valuable you appear — because specialists are rare, and scarcity commands a premium.

Test: Could someone else in your field copy your positioning word for word? If yes, it's not specific enough.

Signal 02 · Premium Language
Not "I help people."
But exactly who
and exactly what.
Language is the first signal of premium — before they even see your work.

The words you use signal your price bracket. Vague language = commodity pricing. Precise, outcome-oriented language = premium pricing.

Commodity: "I help companies with their marketing."
Premium: "I build LinkedIn content systems that generate 20+ qualified leads per month for B2B consultants — without paid ads or cold outreach."

Audit every line of your LinkedIn profile. Replace generic words with specific outcomes, timeframes, and results.

Upgrade formula: Replace "I help with X" → "I help [specific who] achieve [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe]."

Signal 03 · The Price Signal
Talk about results,
never about effort.
Clients pay for outcomes, not hours worked.

Commodity experts describe inputs: "I'll create 12 posts per month." Premium experts describe outputs: "You'll generate consistent inbound enquiries from decision-makers who already trust you."

When you talk about your work, always lead with the result the client experiences, not the deliverable they receive. This reframes your price as an investment in an outcome, not a cost for a service — and that single shift changes every pricing conversation you'll ever have.

Reframe: Don't sell "a content strategy." Sell "a system that makes you the obvious choice in your niche."

Signal 04 · Social Proof Architecture
Show transformation,
not testimonials.
Weak proof: "Great to work with." Strong proof: "Before/after with numbers."

Most social proof is generic. "Amazing results! Highly recommend!" says nothing. Premium proof is specific transformation: "Before working with Kishan, we had 0 inbound leads from LinkedIn. After 90 days, we were booking 8 qualified calls per month."

Structure your proof as: Situation Before → What You Did → Measurable Result After. This format is credible, specific, and tells a story. Put your best proof cases in featured section, posts, and About bio — not buried in recommendations.

Proof hierarchy: Client quotes with numbers > Case study posts > Screenshots > Vague testimonials

Signal 05 · Scarcity Signal
Limited spots.
Clear criteria.
High standards.
Premium positioning includes being selective about who you work with.

Premium experts don't take every client. They take the right clients. Communicating this publicly — "I currently have 2 spots open for Q3 clients" or "I only work with founders doing $1M+" — signals that you are in demand and have standards.

This creates aspiration. Potential clients want to qualify for your services, not just afford them. This is the opposite of desperation marketing. It repositions the relationship: they need to be a good fit for you, not just able to pay.

Apply this: Add clear "ideal client" criteria to your profile. State what you don't do. State who you work best with.
